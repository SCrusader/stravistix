Fitness trend user guide

Principles

In simplified terms, each efforts made involves different levels of fatigue depending on stress you put in. Your body reacts to these solicitations and makes you "stronger" for the following efforts. Through all these efforts you create a "background training base" over time called "fitness". To put numbers on fitness and fatigue over time, every efforts have to be measured. To measure an activity effort, stress scores are being used.

It exists several recognized stress scores calculation methods for an activity depending on available sensors (they are detailed in a further section).
  • Using a Heart Rate Monitor:
    • Training Impulse (TRIMP). Represents a training load based on your activity heart rate data. It depends on exercise duration and intensity.
    • Heart Rate Stress Score (HRSS). Based on TRIMP, HRSS also represents a training load based on your activity heart rate data. HRSS is more accurate than TRIMP it-self. The use of HRSS is recommend over TRIMP in fitness trend. Note that HRSS is equivalent to HrTSS® from TrainingPeaks™
  • Using a Cycling Power Meter:
    • Power Stress Score (PSS). Calculated from workout moving duration and athlete Cycling Functional Threshold Power (FTP). Note that PSS is equivalent to TSS® from TrainingPeaks™
  • Without sensors:
    • Swimming Stress Score (SSS): Calculated from workout moving duration, distance and athlete Swimming Functional Threshold Pace (SFTP). Note that SSS is equivalent to sTSS® from TrainingPeaks™

Each activity having a stress score is a kind of "impulse" causing its own amount of fatigue and fitness background. Fatigue is easy to understand: It's the tired feelings which limits your performance. Fitness is basically the foundations, the potential availability of the athlete... You can't get into big rides/runs or races without a minimum of fitness acquired.

All these "impulses" can be entered in a mathematical impulse-response model which provide a "view" of the fatigue and fitness over time. This model is the "fitness trend" also called "Performance Management Chart" in TrainingPeaks™.

Heart Rate Stress Score (all workout types)

A heart rate stress is represented by a value called Training Impulse or Training Impulse. Training Impulse concept has been developed by Dr. Eric W. Banister. Basically, it represents the amount of heart stress during an activity: the longer you go at full throttle during an activity, the more Training Impulse of activity goes up! Training Impulse has both a positive and a negative effect. The positive effect is called fitness, and the negative effect is called fatigue. Fitness and fatigue can combined to provide a value of form & Performance.

Reading Curves

The Fitness, Fatigue and Form graph and table is based off an impulse-response model first developed by Dr. Eric W. Banister in 1975. It was later redefined by This model will show you:
  • Your Fitness based on your chronic load
  • Your Fatigue based on your acute load
  • Your Form or stress balance
At a glance
  • The Fitness curve is the long-term average daily training load. Default period is fixed to 42 days (or six weeks), so it will take ~6 weeks for your Fitness to be accurate. It's basically the foundations, the potential availability of the athlete... You can't get into big rides/runs or races without a minimum of fitness acquired. If that curve is too regular or too flat, it is not usually a sign of good training.
  • The Fatigue curve is the short-term average daily training load. Same way than Fitness but default period is fixed to 7 days (one week). Conceptually, fatigue is easy to understand: It's the tired feelings which limits your performance. This curve varies much faster than Fitness curve. On Fatigue curve, you can see the fatigue climbing sharply in response where you performed workouts with a high stress. But also go down quickly as you take few days off.
  • The Form curve is simply the difference between Fitness and Fatigue, it's the right balance of stress you should look at to avoid over training for example.
    Form = Fitness - Fatigue
    When:
    • +25 < Form : Transition zone. Athlete is on form. Case where athlete has an extended break. (e.g. illness, injury or end of the season).
    • +5 < Form < +25 : Freshness Zone. Athlete is on form. Ready for a race.
    • -10 < Form < +5 : Neutral Zone. Zone reached typically when athlete is in a rest or recovery week. After a race or hard training period.
    • -30 < Form < -10 : Optimal Training Zone.
    • Form < -30 : Over Load Zone. Athlete is on overload or over-training phase. They should take a rest!
    • Be fit doesn't mean you are ready to race at your higher level. A negative Form would reveal that you are carrying fatigue and are not on form. By resting you can remove fatigue faster than you lose fitness and come into form for your race day (or punish friend day... ;))!
